<Section icon={Sparkles} title="Optimization Strategies for Maximum Profit">

To achieve the best **flip a boat selling prices**, you must treat your restoration like a business, not a hobby. This means sourcing parts at wholesale prices and minimizing "scope creep"—the tendency to fix things that don't actually add to the resale value.

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Strategic Acquisition">
    Look for "divorce sales" or estate liquidations where the goal is a quick exit rather than top dollar. Aim to buy at 40% below market value.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="The 48-Hour Deep Clean">
    Before buying any parts, spend two days cleaning every inch. You might find that the "ruined" gelcoat just needs a heavy compound buff.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Functional Restoration">
    Address all safety items first: bilge pumps, navigation lights, and fuel lines. A boat that doesn't pass a basic inspection won't sell.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="Cosmetic 'Pop'">
    Apply a fresh coat of anti-fouling paint and replace faded decals. These are the visual cues that justify a higher price point.
  </Step>
  <Step num="5" title="Marketing & Staging">
    Take professional photos during the "golden hour." Include a video of the engine running and the boat on plane.
  </Step>
</Steps>

| Restoration Level | Time Investment | Budget | Expected Margin |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Quick Flip** | 20 Hours | $500 | 25% |
| **Standard Refurb** | 60 Hours | $2,500 | 40% |
| **Full Restoration** | 150+ Hours | $7,000+ | 50%+ |

<Callout type="success" title="Pro Strategy">
Focus on the "Three-Foot Rule": If a flaw isn't visible from three feet away, it might not be worth the cost to repair in a mid-range flip. Focus your budget on glaring issues first.
</Callout>
