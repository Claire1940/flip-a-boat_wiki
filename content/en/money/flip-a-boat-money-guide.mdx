<Section icon={Map} title="Sourcing and Inspection Checklist">

Finding the right project is half the battle in this **flip a boat money guide**. You want to avoid "hull rot" at all costs, as fiberglass repair is labor-intensive and expensive. Focus on boats that have been sitting in dry storage or under a "mixed pine and oak" canopy, even if they are covered in debris. A thick layer of dirt can often preserve the gel coat from sun damage, making the final polishing much easier.

When inspecting a potential buy, bring a multimeter and a small jump-pack. Testing the electrical continuity before handing over cash can save you thousands. If the owner claims the engine "ran last year," verify it by checking the oil for milkiness (water intrusion) and the spark plugs for fouling.

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Initial Hull Sounding">
    Tap the hull with a rubber mallet. A sharp "crack" is good; a dull "thud" indicates delamination or water-logged foam.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Electrical Continuity Test">
    Check the main service box. Ensure the 50 amp breakers are intact and there is no green corrosion on the terminals.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Trailer Assessment">
    A boat is useless without a trailer. Check the bearings, tires, and frame for structural rust. A "nice little trailer load" of work is fine, but a snapped axle is a dealbreaker.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="Engine Compression">
    If possible, perform a compression test. Even numbers across all cylinders indicate a healthy block that is worth the restoration effort.
  </Step>
</Steps>
