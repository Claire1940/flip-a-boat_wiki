<Section icon={Sparkles} title="Hull Rotation: How to Flip a Boat for Restoration">

Working on the bottom of a boat hull is incredibly difficult when the vessel is sitting on a trailer. To patch fiberglass, apply bottom paint, or repair gelcoat damage, you will often need to flip the boat upside down. This physical rotation must be executed carefully to prevent structural deformation or personal injury.

Flipping a hull requires a controlled environment with ample overhead clearance. You can use engine hoists, gantry cranes, or a series of heavy-duty straps suspended from overhead beams if they are rated for the weight. Ensure the hull is completely stripped of heavy components like engines, fuel tanks, and loose gear before attempting a flip.

<Steps>
  <Step num="1" title="Strip the Hull">
    Remove the outboard motor, battery, fuel tanks, steering consoles, and all deck hardware to minimize weight.
  </Step>
  <Step num="2" title="Prepare the Rigging">
    Attach heavy-duty lifting straps around the bow eye and transom tie-down points. Ensure all hoist systems are rated for the total weight.
  </Step>
  <Step num="3" title="Execute the Lift">
    Slowly raise the boat off the trailer. Guide the hull manually as it rotates to prevent it from swinging violently.
  </Step>
  <Step num="4" title="Support the Inverted Hull">
    Lower the inverted boat onto padded cradles or wooden sawhorses lined with carpet to protect the gunwales.
  </Step>
</Steps>

Once the boat is flipped, ensure it is stable and level. Any twist in the hull support system can cause the fiberglass to cure in a warped shape during repairs.

<Section icon={Map} title="Mastering Transport: Flip a Boat How to Tow a Boat Safely">

Once the restoration is complete or when you first acquire the project, you must transport the vessel. Understanding **flip a boat how to tow a boat** safely is essential to protect your investment and ensure road safety. Towing requires matching the capacity of your tow vehicle, hitch, and trailer with the total weight of the boat.

The gross trailer weight rating (GTWR) includes the weight of the boat, trailer, fuel, and all onboard gear. Exceeding this limit can cause braking failures, trailer sway, or transmission damage to your tow vehicle.

| Hitch Class | Max Gross Trailer Weight | Max Tongue Weight |
| Class I | Up to 2,000 lbs | 200 lbs |
| Class II | Up to 3,500 lbs | 350 lbs |
| Class III | Up to 5,000 lbs | 500 lbs |
| Class IV | Up to 10,000 lbs | 1,000 lbs |

When coupling the trailer to the tow vehicle, ensure the hitch ball matches the coupler size exactly. Secure the safety chains by crossing them under the trailer tongue in an "X" pattern. This creates a cradle that will catch the tongue if the coupler disconnects from the ball hitch.

<Callout type="info" title="Towing Tip">
Tongue weight should represent 10% to 15% of the total trailer weight. Too little tongue weight causes trailer sway, while too much tongue weight overloads the tow vehicle's rear axle.
</Callout>

</Section>

<Section icon={Shield} title="Essential Gear and Trailer Checklist">

A successful tow depends entirely on the condition of the trailer. Many project boats sit idle for years, leading to dry-rotted tires, rusted leaf springs, and seized wheel bearings. Before taking a newly acquired trailer onto the highway, perform a comprehensive inspection.

Wheel bearings are the most common point of failure for boat trailers. Because boat trailers are regularly submerged in water, moisture can bypass worn seals and rust the bearings, leading to catastrophic failure at highway speeds.
